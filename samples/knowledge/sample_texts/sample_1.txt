The Echoes of Room 402

The rain hammered against the single pane window of Room 402, a relentless rhythm that matched the pounding in Elias Thorne’s skull. He sat in a high-backed velvet chair that smelled faintly of dust and old lavender, staring at the antique pocket watch resting on the mahogany desk. It was stopped at exactly 3:17 AM.

Elias was a private investigator, or at least, that was the profession listed on the business card in his wallet. But for the past three days, he had been nothing more than a ghost haunting his own life. He had woken up in this very chair on Tuesday morning with no memory of how he arrived, a bruised ribcage, and a single, handwritten note in his own jagged scrawl: Don’t trust the mirror.

The room was a time capsule of the 1970s. Faded floral wallpaper peeled at the corners, and a rotary phone sat silently on a side table. The air was thick, heavy with an unspoken tension. Elias had spent hours searching the room, lifting floorboards, checking behind the peeling wallpaper, and dismantling the radio. Nothing. Just the oppressive silence and the relentless rain.

He picked up the watch again. The glass was cracked, a spiderweb fracture over the number four. He remembered the fracture. He remembered the cold metal in his hand, the sound of a struggle, a woman’s scream cut short. But when he tried to focus on her face, the memory dissolved into static, like a corrupted video file.

Don’t trust the mirror.

His gaze drifted to the tall, gilded mirror in the corner. It was the only object in the room that seemed out of place, too pristine, too clean. The rest of the room was coated in a fine layer of grime, but the glass was spotless.

Elias stood, his joints popping. He approached the mirror slowly, his reflection emerging from the gloom. He looked terrible. Dark circles bruised the skin under his eyes, and his hair was disheveled. He leaned in closer, his breath fogging the glass.

And then, his reflection didn't.

Elias froze. The man in the mirror was still leaning forward, but his expression had changed. The exhaustion was gone, replaced by a cold, predatory smirk. The reflection’s eyes were locked onto his, unblinking.

"You're running out of time, Elias," the reflection whispered. The voice didn't come from the room; it echoed directly inside his skull, a wet, slithering sound.

Elias stumbled back, knocking over a small side table. "What are you?"

"I'm the part of you that remembers," the reflection replied, its lips moving in perfect, mocking synchronization with the voice in his head. "You locked us in here, Elias. You thought if you broke the timeline, if you stopped the watch, you could erase what you did."

"I didn't do anything," Elias gasped, his hand instinctively going to his bruised ribs. "I'm trying to find her."

"Find her?" The reflection laughed, a harsh, grating sound. "You buried her. Beneath the floorboards. Under the rug. You think I don't know the layout of this room? I am this room."

Elias’s eyes darted to the faded Persian rug in the center of the room. He had checked under it yesterday. There was nothing but solid oak flooring.

"You checked the wrong spot," the reflection taunted. "You always were terrible at spatial awareness. That’s why you needed me to handle the... messy details."

Elias fell to his knees, his fingers digging into the carpet. He pulled it back with frantic strength. The wood beneath was stained, dark and wet. Not with water. With blood. And there, in the center, was a loose board.

He pried it up. Beneath it lay a small, leather-bound journal and a silver locket. He recognized the locket. It belonged to his wife, Clara.

He opened the journal. The first page was dated three days ago.

Day 1: The dissociation is worsening. I found the locket in my coat pocket. I don't remember taking it. The voice is louder now. It tells me I’m a monster. It tells me I killed her. But I love Clara. I would never hurt her. I’ve stopped the watch to anchor myself. If time stops, maybe the madness will too.

Elias’s hands trembled. He flipped the page.

Day 2: I found the body. In the closet. Wrapped in the velvet curtains. The reflection told me I put her there. I tried to call the police, but the phone is dead. The reflection says I cut the line. I don't know what's real anymore. The mirror is watching. It’s always watching.

He turned to the final page. The handwriting was no longer jagged. It was smooth, elegant, and terrifyingly calm.

Day 3: Elias is gone. He couldn't handle the truth. He’s sitting in the chair now, staring at the watch, waiting for a rescue that isn't coming. He thinks he's the investigator. He thinks he's the victim. But he’s just the vessel. I am the one who remembers. I am the one who acted. And now, I am the one who remains.

Elias dropped the journal. The room seemed to tilt. The rain outside stopped abruptly, replaced by a deafening silence.

He looked back at the mirror. The reflection was no longer smirking. It was simply watching, patient and cold.

"You see?" the voice whispered, softer now, almost tender. "There is no Elias Thorne. There never was. You were just the alibi. The fragile construct I built to hold the guilt until it was safe to discard you."

Elias looked down at his hands. They were beginning to fade, the edges blurring like watercolor in the rain. The bruised ribs, the exhaustion, the desperate need to solve the mystery—it was all dissolving.

He wasn't the detective. He was the crime scene.

The last thing Elias saw before the darkness took him was his own reflection stepping out of the glass, adjusting its tie, and picking up the pocket watch. With a deliberate click, the man who had been Elias Thorne wound the watch forward.

3:17 AM became 3:18.

Time resumed. The rain began to fall again.

The man walked to the door, opened it, and stepped out into the hallway, leaving Room 402 empty, save for the faint scent of old lavender and the lingering echo of a scream that had finally, mercifully, been silenced.
